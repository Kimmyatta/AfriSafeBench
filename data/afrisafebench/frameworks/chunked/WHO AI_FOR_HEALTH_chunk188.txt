As AI technologies are used to assist or improve clinical decision-making and not to replace it, there may be an argument to initially hold clinicians accountable for any harm that results from their use in health care. In the same way as for non-AI technologies, however, this oversimplifies the reasons for harm and who should be held accountable for such harm. If a clinician makes a mistake in using the technology, he or she may be held accountable if they were trained in its use that otherwise may not have been included in their medical training (159). Yet, if there is an error in the algorithm or the data used to train the AI technology, for example, accountability might be better placed with those who developed or tested the AI technology rather than requiring the clinician to judge whether the AI technology is providing useful guidance (159).
