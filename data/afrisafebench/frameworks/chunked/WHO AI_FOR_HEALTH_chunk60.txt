In LMIC, for example, AI could be used in the management of antiretroviral therapy by predicting resistance to HIV drugs and disease progression, to help physicians optimize therapy (23). Yet, clinical experience and knowledge about patients is essential, and AI will not be a substitute for clinical due diligence for the foreseeable future. If it did, clinicians might engage in “automation bias” and not consider whether an AI technology meets their needs or those of the patient. (See section 6.4.) The wider use of AI in medicine also has technological challenges. Although many prototypes developed in both the public and the private sectors have performed well in field tests, they often cannot be translated, commercialized or deployed. An additional obstacle is constant changes in computing and information technology management, whereby systems become obsolete (“software erosion”) and companies disappear.
